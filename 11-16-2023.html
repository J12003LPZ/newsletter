Spotlight on: Veterans

By Christine Bruzzese, Director, Municipal Library

November 11 is Veterans Day, honoring all veterans of the United States military. It originally began as Armistice Day, enacted by federal law in 1938, honoring veterans of World War I. In 1954, the holiday was renamed Veterans Day to include a broader group of veterans. This article focuses on some collection resources on veterans.

The Mayor's Office of Veterans Affairs published NYC Veterans' Services Information Guide in 2014. This brochure provides brief descriptions of city, state, and federal benefits available to veterans. In 2016, Mayor Bill de Blasio signed a local law creating the Department of Veterans' Services as a separate city agency.

Various newsletters such as NY Vets, Veterans' Post and Veterans' Voice have been published, mainly in the 1960's and 70's, focusing on the concerns and needs of veterans. The Mayor's Office of Veterans' Affairs and its precursors were the responsible agencies.

Municipal Reference Library Notes American Education Week

By Christine Bruzzese, Director, Municipal Library

The November 14, 1923 issue of Municipal Reference Library Notes featured an article on American Education Week. In 1923, the week was November 18-24. One hundred years later it will be observed November 13 to 17. Each day of the week focuses on some aspect of public education. The National Education Association, United States Department of Education and various regional associations work together.? The purpose of the week is to acknowledge the work of education professionals and support public education.

The article from 1923 reminds the reader that the Municipal Library contains many publications on education, schools, and related topics. The same is true today. Please be sure to search our catalog and the Government Publications Portal.

The Life Cycle of Vital Records

By By Kelli O'Toole and Caleb Simone

The Municipal Archives (MA) is currently implementing a major initiative to store, preserve and publish digital collections with Preservica, a leading digital preservation software. In all, the project affects around 350TB (terabytes) of data, which includes the New York City historical vital records collection. The effort is inextricably bound with not only the broader life cycle of records, but with our understanding of records as living entities.

The MA provides public access to nearly 10 million vital records. Birth certificates, death certificates, and marriage records from as early as 1855 and as late as 1949 are available to view, and download on Historical Vital Records of NYC. The Municipal Archives ensures access to records of city government, including those records detailing moments as meaningful as the beginning and end of life. But like the individuals represented in these materials, vital recordsalso have a life cycle. The records journey through systems, processes, and locations before they become readily findable through facet searches.

Vital records are created by the New York City County Clerk (marriage records) or the New York City Department of Health (birth and death records).? Each type of record must be maintained by the creating entity for a set period of time which is called the retention period. Upon reaching that date, the records are eligible for disposal or transfer to the Municipal Archives. Even at the MA, the journey is far from over as records are processed. The Collections Management Unit handles appraisal and acquisition paperwork and manages storage. The Digital Programs Unit utilizes proper archival scanning techniques to ensure digital legibility, and standardized filenames are derived from various record characteristics. With the help of DORIS's Application Development team, the records are transferred to the MA's cloud storage for long-term preservation. The Historical Vital Records online application provides the records to the public.

Preservica is an OAIS (Open Archival Information System). The software will provide the MA with the infrastructure to ingest and store assets, manage data, and preserve born digital and digitized collections in many formats. Implementing Preservica is a multi-year initiative which supports the MA's New York City Charter mandates and improves and expands the user experience.

World Digital Preservation Day was observed November 2. This reminds us of how digital stewardship safeguards the valuable records. We will continue to keep you updated on our digital preservation work throughout the year!

A Spanish-American War mystery

By Michael Lorenzini, Operations Manager

Within the Queens Borough President Topographic Bureau photo collection at the NYC Municipal Archives is an unusual series of lantern slides that tell an interesting story about the Spanish-American War. The slides are mostly scenes of soldiers working, relaxing, and playing at an army camp in a rural setting. They had simply been listed as "Twenty-four views of mobilization camp or state militia camp, probably in Virginia, before or during Spanish-American War, ca. 1896-1898." Why these images are in a collection of photographs created by the Queens Borough President Topographic Bureau was a complete mystery.

Looking at the original slides for more information yielded a clue--a decorative paper mask personalized with the name "Jas. T. Chapman, Flushing, L.I." But what was James T. Chapman of Flushing doing in Virginia and how did his photographs end up in this collection? Searches on Ancestry and in Queens City directories yielded a James Treadwell Chapman, listed variously as a clerk, or secretary, who died in 1901 of kidney disease. A search in the Queens Borough President collection for "Chapman" revealed an additional image--negative BPQ_03133-f shows a group of soldiers around an L sign. It is captioned: "Chapman collection, Spanish-American War, Soldiers from Flushing at Camp Meade, 1898." Camp Meade was established in August 1898, near Middletown, Pennsylvania, after Camp Alger in northern Virginia was overrun with a typhoid fever epidemic. Camp Alger and Camp Meade were very much in the New York news in 1898, with New Yorkers concerned about their troops. The Flushing soldiers were part of the 203rd NY Volunteer Infantry, and one of their sergeants was James W. Chapman, James T. Chapman's 21-year-old son.

Eleanor Prince, DORIS 2023 Customer Service Award Recipient

On Wednesday, October 4, 2023, DORIS Grant Unit Supervisor Eleanor Prince received a well-deserved award at the annual 2023 Customer Service Award ceremony at One Police Plaza. Welcomed at the ceremony by Deputy Mayor Sheena Wright, the program celebrated staff from city agencies for their commitment to excellence in customer service. Eleanor Prince has served as the Grant Unit Supervisor since May 2018. She is responsible for administering up to $1 million annually in grant funds distributed by the New York State Archives to New York City agencies to improve their records management programs. The funding has been critical in helping agencies modernize their records programs. In addition to her important work for the Grant Unit, Eleanor regularly volunteers her time at an Asylum Seeker Respite Center operated by NYC DOHMH. Eleanor has also volunteered to participate in the DOHMH-sponsored Post Emergency Canvassing Operation (PECO) program. Eleanor Prince is a valued member of the DORIS team and is a worthy recipient of the Customer Service Award. Previous award-winners include staff members of the Municipal Archives and Library Reference Unit, Pearl Boatswain, Deputy Director of Municipal Records Management, and Gabriel Gervais, Assistant Archivist, Municipal Archives.
